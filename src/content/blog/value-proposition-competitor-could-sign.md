Try this test on your own website. Copy your value proposition into a document. Then paste the value propositions of your three closest competitors right underneath. Strip the logos. Now read all four and try to tell them apart.

If you can't, you have a problem. And it's the most expensive problem in B2B, because it's the one nobody notices.

Here's the real diagnostic, sharper than any positioning workshop. Take your homepage headline and imagine your biggest competitor putting their logo on it. Would anything become false? Would anyone at their company object? If the answer is no, if a rival could sign your value proposition and mean every word, then what you have isn't a value proposition. It's positioning theatre. It looks like a stance. It commits to nothing.

## The Sameness Test Most B2B Companies Fail

"Trusted partner." "End-to-end solutions." "Innovative, customer-centric, results-driven." Read enough B2B homepages and they blur into one long sentence written by a committee that has never met a specific customer.

The words feel safe because they're true. You *are* trusted. You *do* deliver solutions. But true and differentiating are not the same thing. Every claim on that list is true for your competitors too. A statement that applies equally to everyone in your category carries exactly zero information for the buyer trying to choose. It's noise dressed up as a message.

The uncomfortable part is that this sameness isn't an accident of lazy copywriting. It's the predictable output of how most companies actually make positioning decisions. The blandness is a symptom. The disease is upstream.

## Why It Happens: The Machinery of Sameness

![Four abstract shapes converging into one identical form](../../assets/blog/value-proposition-01.webp)

Nobody sits in a meeting and says "let's sound exactly like everyone else." Sameness gets manufactured by four forces, each one reasonable on its own.

**The fear of narrowing.** Choosing who you're *for* means choosing who you're *not* for, and that feels like leaving money on the table. So the value proposition gets stretched wide enough to welcome everyone. A message built to exclude nobody ends up meaning nothing to anybody. Width is the enemy of memory.

**Committee wording.** Positioning by consensus is a machine for removing edges. Sales wants it broader so no deal feels off-limits. Legal wants the bold claim softened. A VP wants their pet segment included. Every round of feedback sands off another sharp corner, and what survives is the smooth, inoffensive average that survives precisely *because* it offends no internal stakeholder. The problem is that internal stakeholders aren't your buyers.

**Copying the category leader.** When one company defines the category, everyone else instinctively echoes their language to seem credible, to signal "we play in this space too." But echoing the leader doesn't borrow their authority. It confirms you're the alternative nobody had to consider. You can't out-leader the leader by using their own words.

**Confusing "what we do" with "why we're the only sane choice."** Most value propositions describe a category, not a position. "We help B2B companies grow pipeline" is what you do. It's not a reason to pick you over the eleven other firms that say the identical thing. Describing your function is table stakes. Positioning starts where the description ends.

## What Real Positioning Actually Requires

Here's the idea most teams flinch from: positioning is a set of trade-offs. Not a list of strengths. Trade-offs.

This isn't a hot take, it's the foundation of strategy. Michael Porter has argued for decades that the essence of strategy is choosing what *not* to do. A position is defined as much by what it rejects as by what it claims. If your value proposition has no downside, if there's no type of customer it deliberately turns away, then you haven't taken a position. You've written a wish.

Real positioning means being explicitly wrong for some buyers so you can be obviously right for others. The premium option is wrong for the price-sensitive buyer, on purpose. The specialist is wrong for the company that wants one vendor for everything, on purpose. That "wrongness" isn't a flaw in the positioning. It's the proof that positioning exists at all.

The practical test: does your value proposition contain a claim someone could reasonably disagree with? A point of view a competitor might actively reject? If everyone in your market would nod along, you've described a consensus, and consensus is invisible. Specificity beats safety every single time, because specificity is the only thing a buyer can actually remember and repeat to a colleague.

## A Practical Path Out of Sameness

![A single distinct shape standing apart from a cluster of identical ones](../../assets/blog/value-proposition-02.webp)

You don't fix this with a thesaurus and better adjectives. You fix it by making the hard choices the committee has been avoiding. Four moves.

**Name who you're NOT for.** Write it down, literally. "This is wrong for companies that want X." The moment you can name who you turn away, the people you're *for* recognize themselves instantly. Exclusion is what makes inclusion feel personal.

**Name the enemy.** Not a competitor. The status quo you're replacing, the broken default your buyer tolerates today. Great positioning always has a villain: the bloated incumbent, the "we've always done it this way," the expensive tool nobody actually uses. Naming what you're against tells buyers what you're for, faster than any feature list.

**Claim one thing you'd stake the business on.** Not five differentiators. One. The single conviction you'd defend when a prospect pushes back, the thing you believe that your competitors either don't believe or won't say out loud. If you can't reduce it to one sentence you'd bet on, you haven't found it yet.

**Pressure-test every line with the signature test.** For each claim on your homepage, ask: could a competitor say this too, and mean it? If yes, cut it or sharpen it until the answer is no. Run the whole page through that filter and watch how much "safe" copy turns out to be dead weight.

This is the core of what we do in [brand strategy](/services/brand-strategy) work, and it's less about clever writing than about making decisions the organization has been dodging. The words are easy once the choices are made.

## Differentiation Is the Willingness to Be Disliked

Here's the part that makes people nervous, and it's the whole game. Differentiation is the willingness to be disliked by the wrong customers.

A value proposition that everyone finds agreeable is a value proposition that no one finds compelling. The moment your positioning is sharp enough to make the wrong-fit buyer think "that's not for me," it becomes sharp enough to make the right-fit buyer think "finally, someone gets it." You can't have the second reaction without accepting the first. They're the same edge, cutting in two directions.

So go back to the test. Put your value proposition next to your competitors'. If they could sign it, it was never yours to begin with. The goal isn't to be liked by the whole market. It's to be undeniable to a specific slice of it, and quietly, deliberately, wrong for the rest.
