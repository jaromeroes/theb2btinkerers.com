Here's the most expensive assumption in B2B marketing: that the people you're selling to are ready to buy. Almost none of them are. And the whole machinery of lead-gen is built as if they were. Brand-building is the part of marketing that accepts the opposite, that most of your future customers aren't in the market today, and does something about it anyway. It's the work you can't see on this quarter's dashboard. It's also, over any horizon that matters, the work that wins.

Let me explain why, because this is the argument that decides where your budget actually goes.

## The 95% you're pretending don't exist

There's a finding from the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute, put clearly by John Dawes, that should reframe how you think about every campaign you run. It's called the 95-5 rule. At any given moment, only about 5% of B2B buyers are actually in-market, meaning they have a live need, a budget, and an intention to buy something in your category. The other 95% aren't. They're not evaluating vendors. They're not filling out forms. They're running their business and not thinking about you at all.

Now look at where most B2B marketing money goes. Demand capture. Paid search on high-intent keywords. Retargeting. Gated content designed to catch a hand-raise. Sales-led nurture sequences that fire the moment someone downloads a whitepaper. All of it is aimed, with great precision, at that 5%. And all of it competes, head to head, with every other vendor doing exactly the same thing to exactly the same tiny pool.

This is the uncomfortable math nobody says out loud in the QBR. You can have the sharpest lead-gen engine on earth, and it still can't convert someone who isn't buying. No demo, no discount, no sequence of seven perfectly-timed emails turns a 95%-er into a customer this quarter. They're not resisting your pitch. They're just not in the market. Pushing harder doesn't shorten a sales cycle that hasn't started.

## What brand does that lead-gen structurally cannot

So what do you do with 95% of your market who can't be converted today? You could ignore them, which is what most budgets quietly do. Or you could do the one thing that actually matters: make sure that when they *do* enter the market, next quarter or next year, you're already the name in their head.

That's the entire job of brand-building. Not awareness for its own sake, not logo impressions to feel good about. It's the slow, deliberate construction of memory in people who aren't ready yet, so that at the exact moment a need appears, you're the first company they think of and the easiest one to justify choosing. Buyers don't build a shortlist from scratch when the need hits. They pull from a mental shortlist they've been assembling, half-consciously, for months or years. Brand-building is how you get on that list before the buying even starts.

This is the marketing you can't see. It doesn't produce a lead today. It produces a bias, a slight tilt toward you, in a stranger who won't act for eighteen months. Which is exactly why it's so easy to defund, and exactly why the companies that hold their nerve on it pull away from the ones that don't. The effect compounds. Every quarter you build memory in the 95% is a quarter of future pipeline you're pre-loading, and it accumulates in a way that a lead-gen spend, which resets to zero the day you turn it off, never does.

## Why the wrong thing wins the argument anyway

If brand-building is so powerful, why does it lose the budget fight almost every time? Because of one brutal asymmetry: lead-gen attributes cleanly and brand doesn't.

You can draw a straight line from a paid search click to a form fill to a closed deal. It shows up in the dashboard. It has a CPA, a ROAS, a number your CFO can point at. Brand-building has none of that. Its payoff is diffuse, delayed, and tangled up with everything else that happened over the same period. You cannot prove that the deal you closed today came from the memory you built two years ago, even when it did. So in every budget conversation, the measurable thing beats the important thing. Not because it's worth more. Because it's easier to defend.

This is the tyranny of last-click, and it quietly warps the whole function. Marketers optimize for what they can report, which pushes spend relentlessly toward the bottom of the funnel, toward the 5%, toward the metrics that light up a slide. The strategic work, the brand-building that would actually grow the business, has no angry attribution model demanding it happen. So it's forever the thing that gets cut first when the quarter looks tight. Perpetually important, never provable, always on the chopping block. If that pattern sounds familiar, it's the same short-termism we've written about in [why your marketing team is always firefighting](/insights): the measurable, urgent thing crowds out the important one, every single time.

## The answer isn't brand OR lead-gen. It's the ratio.

Here's where people overcorrect. They read the 95-5 rule, get religion about brand, and swing to the other extreme, all long-term, no capture. That's just as broken. You still need to convert the 5% who are in-market right now. If you don't, you're building memory you never cash in.

The most useful guidance we have comes from Les Binet and Peter Field, who spent years analyzing what actually drives long-term growth versus short-term response. Their finding, roughly, is that the optimal split is around 60% of budget on brand-building and 40% on activation, on the lead-gen and demand capture that convert existing intent. Sixty on the future, forty on the now. Brand builds the demand; activation harvests it. Starve either side and the machine stalls.

Now, be honest about your own situation before you apply that number. The 60/40 benchmark comes largely from big-budget, established brands, and if you're a smaller B2B company still fighting to be known at all, your reality is different. You may need more weight on capture in the near term simply to keep the lights on and the pipeline moving. That's fine. The point isn't to copy the ratio to the decimal. The point is to *have* a ratio, deliberately, out loud, instead of letting last-click attribution quietly drag you to 95% activation and 5% brand without anyone ever deciding that on purpose. Pick your split, defend it, and hold it through the quarters when the dashboard begs you to raid the brand budget for one more capture push.

The patience is the strategy. Brand-building only compounds if you let it run long enough to compound. Fund it for a quarter, panic when it doesn't produce leads, and kill it, and you've proven nothing except that you didn't understand what you bought.

## The bet worth making

The best marketing you'll ever do is a bet on a buyer who isn't ready yet. It won't show up in this quarter's numbers. It won't have a clean CPA. It'll look, to anyone watching only the dashboard, like money that didn't do anything. And then eighteen months from now a stranger who never filled out a form will put you on their shortlist first, and won't quite be able to tell you why.

That's not a rounding error in your plan. That's where durable growth comes from. Lead-gen fishes the 5% everyone else is also fishing. Brand-building owns the 95% before they start looking. One is a cost you pay every quarter forever. The other is an asset you build once and keep drawing on. Choose accordingly.
