Open next year's marketing budget. There's a line for the trade show. You know the one. Same hall, same week of the calendar, same booth footprint, roughly the same number with a bit added for inflation. Now answer honestly: who decided to go?

Not who approved it. Who *decided*.

Most likely, nobody. Not this year, anyway. You're going because you went last year, and last year you went because you went the year before. Somewhere back at the origin, a real person had a real reason that made sense at the time. Then it stopped being examined. What started as a decision quietly turned into a ritual. And that ritual is frequently the single largest line item in the B2B marketing budget, and reliably the one with the least thinking behind it.

To be clear: this isn't an argument that B2B events don't work. Some work extraordinarily well, and I'll make that case properly further down. It's an argument that most companies never actually decide to attend. They just renew.

## The Ritual Nobody Re-Decides

Watch how the trade show enters the plan. It doesn't get proposed, debated, and won. It's already there. It arrives in the budget template as a pre-existing fact, and the only conversation anyone has about it is logistical. Which hall. How big a stand. Who's flying out. Whether we do the sponsored session again.

Notice what's missing from that list. Nobody asks whether to go at all.

This is what separates the event line from everything else in your plan. A new campaign has to justify itself. Someone has to stand up, make a case, and survive the questions. The trade show skips that entirely, because it isn't perceived as a new spend. It's perceived as a renewal. And renewals don't get interrogated, they get processed. Your finance team will scrutinize a $15,000 (€14,000) content project harder than a six-figure hall you've booked eight years running, purely because one is new and the other is already on the shelf.

The tell is what happens when you ask the team why you go. The answers are all backward-looking. "Our customers are there." "We've always had a presence." "It'd look strange if we didn't show up." Each may even be true. None of them is a hypothesis. They're descriptions of the past being used as a plan for the future.

## Why It Survives Every Budget Cut

Here's the part that should bother you. When the quarter tightens and the red pen comes out, the event line is almost never the one that gets cut. Content gets trimmed. The paid test gets paused. Headcount gets frozen. The booth survives.

There are three reasons, and none of them are about performance.

**It's visible.** The CEO walks the booth. They shake hands, they see a crowded stand, they feel the energy of the room. That is a vivid, physical, memorable experience of marketing existing. No dashboard competes with that. A nurture sequence that quietly moves twelve accounts toward a decision produces no such moment, which is exactly why it gets cut first.

**It feels like activity.** There's a stand to build, a schedule to run, badges to print, a team in motion. It generates enormous visible effort, and visible effort reads as productivity. Nobody looks at a team frantically prepping a booth and thinks "is this the highest-value use of the quarter."

**Its returns are conveniently unmeasurable.** This is the real one. When you ask what the show produced, the answer arrives in a language that can't be checked: brand, presence, relationships, being seen in the market. Those are real things. Brand-building genuinely compounds, and I'd defend that in any room. But notice the structure of the claim. It can never be wrong. If pipeline shows up, the show worked. If pipeline doesn't show up, the show was building brand and relationships that will pay off eventually. There is no result that disproves it.

Unfalsifiable spending is the safest spending in the building. It cannot be attacked because it cannot be tested. And that safety is precisely the problem, because "can't be proven wrong" is doing an awful lot of work in a budget that's supposed to be allocating finite money to its best use.

## The Arithmetic Nobody Runs

![Overhead view of a vast, mostly empty exhibition hall at dawn before the crowds arrive](../../assets/blog/trade-shows-01.webp)

Let's do the honest math. Not research, not a benchmark, just an illustrative example of one mid-sized company at one industry show. Your numbers will differ. Run yours.

Booth space, a modest stand at a decent industry event: $30,000 (€27,000). Design, build, furniture, shipping in, shipping out: $35,000 (€32,000). Flights, hotels, per diems for eight people over four days: $20,000 (€18,000). Collateral, giveaways, the good coffee machine, the sponsored session upgrade: $15,000 (€14,000).

Subtotal: $100,000 (€91,000). That's the number in the budget line, and it's already the biggest thing on the page.

Now add what the budget line never shows. Eight people are off their normal work for a week. Two of them are your best AEs, who are not touching pipeline while they're standing on carpet. Realistically it's not one week either, it's a week of prep before and a week of follow-up after, spread across a smaller group. Load those salaries properly and you're adding roughly $30,000 (€27,000) in people cost that appears nowhere in the event budget.

So the real figure is around $130,000 (€118,000). And then there's the line that never gets written down at all: what that same money and that same week of your best people would have produced concentrated on one focused motion for a quarter. That's the actual comparison, and skipping it is how the habit stays alive.

Against that, what comes back? Usually a spreadsheet of badge scans. Say it plainly: badge scans are not pipeline. A badge scan is a record that a human walked within a metre of a device. It contains no intent, no need, no budget, and no timeline. Counting them and calling it a result is how a $130,000 (€118,000) decision gets reported as a success without anyone lying.

## The Case For Events Is Real

![A close, quiet conversation between two people at a small table away from a busy hall](../../assets/blog/trade-shows-02.webp)

Now the fair part, because the honest position here isn't "stop going to events."

Events do something no other channel does. They compress into three days a set of conversations that would take nine months to arrange over video, with people who are physically present, out of their inbox, and in a mood to talk. When the goal is specific, the return can be outstanding.

Three cases where I'd argue *for* the spend without hesitation. When you have a named target-account list and the buying committee is walking that floor, an event is the cheapest way to get in front of people who won't take your call. When you're launching, and the press, analysts, and partners who shape your category are concentrated in one building for seventy-two hours, that concentration is worth paying for. And when you need deep customer research, the kind you can't get on a scheduled call because people are performing, the hallway conversation at hour six of day two will tell you more about your market than a quarter of survey data.

Look at what those three have in common. Each one has a defined goal, a named audience, and a result you'd recognize if you got it. The failure mode isn't the event. It's going without a hypothesis, and then reverse-engineering a justification from whatever happened to occur.

## Decide Before You Book

The fix is unglamorous and takes about an hour. Do it before the deposit, because after the deposit you're not deciding, you're rationalizing.

**Write the hypothesis.** One sentence, in the same format you'd demand of any campaign: "We're going because we believe X will happen, and it matters because Y." If you can't write it, you don't have a reason to go. You have a habit with a booking reference.

**Write the disqualifier.** This is the one everybody skips, and it's the one that makes the whole thing real. Before you book, define what would tell you this was the wrong call. If no evidence could ever count against going, you're back in unfalsifiable territory and you haven't made a decision at all. A hypothesis without a disqualifier is a wish.

**Pre-book meetings with named accounts, or don't go.** This is the sharpest test available. Set a floor: "twelve confirmed meetings with accounts from the target list before we commit to the stand." Then work to hit it. If you can't get twelve target accounts to agree to twenty minutes when they're already going to be in the building with nothing to lose, that's not a scheduling problem. That's the market telling you something about your relevance, and it's worth far more than the show.

**Measure the things that can be wrong.** Meetings held with target accounts. Opportunities created or advanced. Pipeline influenced within a defined window. Not badge scans. Not booth traffic. Not "great energy on the stand." Pick metrics that could come back bad, because a metric that can only come back good isn't measuring anything. If you're not sure your current numbers survive that test, that's usually a sign the whole spend map needs [an honest look](/services/marketing-audit), not just the event line.

## You Never Decided

The question was never whether trade shows work. Plenty of them do, for companies that turned up with a purpose and left with the thing they came for. The question is whether you ever decided to go, or whether you inherited an obligation from a version of the company that made the call under conditions that no longer apply.

Renewal isn't a decision. It's the absence of one, wearing a decision's clothes. And the most expensive habits are never the ones you can't afford. They're the ones you can, which is exactly why nobody ever stops to check.

So this year, before the deposit goes out, ask the question that hasn't been asked in eight years: if this weren't already in the budget, would we put it there?
