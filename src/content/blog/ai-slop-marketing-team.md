There is a specific kind of meeting that marketing leaders are having more and more often. Someone from finance, or sales, or the CEO's office shares something they built over a weekend. A full competitive analysis. A product launch brief. A content calendar for the next quarter. It looks complete. It has structure, headings, bullet points. It took them maybe forty minutes.

Then comes the implication, sometimes stated and sometimes not: why does your team need three weeks for something like this?

This is the AI slop problem. And it is not going away.

## What AI slop actually is

The term is blunt but accurate. AI slop is content or strategic output that looks finished but isn't. It has the shape of thinking without the substance of it. It answers the question that was asked rather than the question that needed to be asked. It is confident, fluent, and frequently wrong in ways that are hard to spot if you don't already know the answer.

The issue is not that the AI produced something bad. The issue is that the output looks good enough to someone who doesn't know what good actually looks like in your specific context: your market, your positioning, your customers, your sales cycle, your brand voice built over years of iteration.

A competitive analysis generated in forty minutes will identify the right competitors. It will describe their products accurately. It will probably miss the thing your Sales Director learned last quarter from three lost deals. It will not capture the positioning nuance that took your team two years to understand. It will not reflect the segment you are actually targeting as opposed to the segment your competitors publicly talk about.

It looks like insight. It isn't.

## Why this is a marketing problem specifically

Every function in a company is being affected by AI. Finance teams are using it. Product teams are using it. Operations teams are using it. But most of those outputs are either verifiable against data or contained within a workflow where quality control exists.

Marketing outputs are different. A financial model either reconciles or it doesn't. A legal brief either cites the right precedents or it doesn't. A piece of positioning, a brand narrative, a campaign concept: the quality is much harder to evaluate without deep context. Which means the slop is harder to catch, and the bar gets set by whoever produces something first.

If a non-marketer produces a positioning document in forty minutes and presents it in a leadership meeting, it becomes the reference point. Your team then has to argue against something that already exists rather than build something from first principles. That is a structural disadvantage, and it compounds over time.

## The real cost is not quality. It is coherence.

Individual AI-generated assets can be good. A well-prompted brief, a first draft of a product page, a framework for segmenting a new market: these are legitimate uses of the technology and they genuinely accelerate execution when a trained marketer is in the loop.

The problem is coherence across all of them.

Brand voice is not a style guide. It is a consistent set of choices about what you say, how you say it, what you do not say, and why. It is built through hundreds of small decisions made by people who understand the brand deeply. An AI tool given a style guide will approximate that voice. It will not hold the line when a new product brief pushes against it, or when a regional team wants to adapt messaging for a different market, or when the Sales Director asks for a one-pager that sounds more aggressive.

Coherence requires judgment. Judgment requires context that cannot be fully encoded in a prompt. And the accumulated effect of many individually acceptable AI outputs, each produced by different people in different contexts with different prompts, is a brand that slowly stops sounding like itself.

This is not theoretical. It is already happening in most mid-market B2B companies that have given their teams broad access to AI tools without a framework for using them.

## What the marketing team's job actually is

The conversation about AI in marketing tends to focus on speed and volume. How many assets can you produce. How fast can you turn a brief into a campaign. How much can you reduce the cost per piece of content.

These are real efficiency gains. But they are inputs, not outcomes.

The job of a B2B marketing team is not to produce assets. It is to build a consistent, credible presence in a market that moves a specific set of buyers from awareness to consideration to preference to purchase. That requires a through-line of thinking that connects every asset to every other asset, and connects all of them to a commercial objective.

AI accelerates execution. It does not replace the thinking that determines what to execute, in what order, for whom, with what message, and why.

The teams that will use AI well are the ones that invest in that thinking first and use AI to scale the execution of it. The teams that will struggle are the ones that invert that order: use AI to produce output fast, then try to find the strategy inside the output afterward.

## What to actually do about it

This is not an argument against using AI. It is an argument for using it with a clear understanding of what it can and cannot hold.

Three things that make the difference in practice.

**Define what requires human judgment before you start.** Positioning, messaging hierarchy, campaign concept, audience segmentation: these are not tasks to hand off. They are the work that makes every downstream asset coherent. Build that layer with your team, then use AI to execute against it.

**Create a shared brief that travels.** The biggest coherence risk is multiple people prompting independently with no shared context. A well-constructed brief, with positioning, voice rules, audience definition, and campaign objectives, used as the input for every AI-assisted task, dramatically reduces drift. It also makes it much easier to catch when something has gone off-brand.

**Establish visible quality standards.** The AI slop problem partly exists because the standard for "good enough" has shifted downward without anyone deciding that it should. Marketing leadership needs to make explicit what good looks like, be specific about where AI output requires review, and hold that line even when the pressure to move fast is real.

None of this is complicated. But it requires someone in the marketing function to own it, and it requires the credibility to have that conversation with the CEO and Sales Director who are excited about the forty-minute deliverable.

That is, in many ways, the more important capability for a B2B marketing leader right now. Not prompt engineering. Strategic clarity about where the value of the function actually lives, and the ability to defend it without sounding like you are defending the past.
