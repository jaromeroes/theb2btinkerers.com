Open your homepage and read the first thing above the fold. If it's a version of "the leading platform for [category] with AI-powered [capability]," I already know something about your pipeline: it's harder to fill than it should be. Because nobody buys your product. Nobody has ever bought a product. They buy the problem it makes go away, and right now your messaging is making them do the translation work themselves.

This is the quietest, most expensive mistake in B2B marketing. Not bad design, not a small budget. A perfectly competent site that talks fluently about itself and says almost nothing about the person reading it.

## The Mirror Problem

Most B2B companies build their marketing like a mirror. It reflects the product back at whoever's looking. Features, specs, modules, the word "platform" three times per paragraph, a capabilities grid, an integrations list. All technically true. All completely centered on the seller.

The buyer walks in carrying a problem. A number they're behind on. A process that keeps breaking. A boss asking why last quarter slipped. They look at your mirror and see... your product. Not themselves, not their situation, not the thing keeping them up at night. So they do a quick, mostly unconscious calculation: "Is this relevant to me?" And because you made them work to answer that, most of them decide it isn't and leave.

You didn't lose because your product was worse. You lost because you described the thing you sell instead of the problem they came to solve. Those are not the same message, and the gap between them is where deals quietly die.

## People Don't Want a Drill. They Want a Hole.

Theodore Levitt said it decades ago and it still gets misquoted in every pitch deck: people don't want a quarter-inch drill, they want a quarter-inch hole. Clayton Christensen took the idea further with Jobs-to-be-Done. His frame is that customers don't really "buy" products. They "hire" them to make progress in a specific situation. The job is the thing they're trying to get done. The product is just the current best candidate for it.

In B2B this is even more true, because nobody is buying with their own money and everyone is buying with their own reputation. Reframe the drill for a second. Nobody wants your data platform. They want to stop being the department that can't answer the CEO's question by Friday. Nobody wants your security tool. They want to stop lying awake about the breach that ends their career. Nobody wants your automation suite. They want their team to stop drowning so the strategic project finally ships.

The job is emotional and social as much as functional. There's the practical outcome, yes. But there's also the risk of choosing wrong, the status of looking competent to the board, the relief of a problem finally handled. When your messaging speaks only to the functional layer, you're addressing maybe a third of what's actually driving the decision.

And notice what this does to who your product even is. The same tool can be hired for completely different jobs by different buyers, and the winning message names the job, not the tool. A reporting product hired to survive the board meeting is a different promise than the same product hired to catch fraud faster, even though the feature list on both pages would be identical. When you lead with the product, you flatten all of those jobs into one generic pitch that fits none of them well. When you lead with the job, you suddenly sound like you were built for exactly the person reading.

## Why Feature-Led Marketing Quietly Fails

Feature-led messaging fails for two reasons, and both are boring, which is exactly why they go unfixed for years.

The first is translation cost. When you lead with features, you're handing the buyer homework. "Here are our capabilities, now figure out which of your problems they solve." Some buyers, the ones already deep in evaluation, will do that work. The vast majority, the ones still deciding whether you're even worth a demo, won't. They don't bounce because they concluded you can't help. They bounce because you asked them to prove it themselves and they had eleven other tabs open.

The second is interchangeability. The moment you define yourself by your feature list, you've volunteered to be compared line by line against every competitor who lists the same features. You become a spreadsheet row. And on a spreadsheet, the only axis anyone can actually read is price. Feature parity is a trap: the closer your capabilities look to everyone else's, the more the decision collapses onto the one thing you least want it to. Problem-led messaging is how you climb out of the spreadsheet, because the way you frame the job, the outcome, and the stakes is much harder to copy than a checkbox.

## How to Find the Real Job

You don't find the real job in a positioning workshop with sticky notes and your own team guessing. You find it by talking to people who recently bought, ideally in the awkward weeks right after they signed, while the memory is fresh.

Ask them about the switch. What was actually happening the week they decided to look for something? Something is always the trigger. A miss, a scare, a new mandate, a competitor doing something that made the status quo feel dangerous. That moment tells you the real job, not the rationalized one they'll give you if you ask "why did you buy."

Then map the forces, which is the underused half of Jobs-to-be-Done. Two things pull them toward change: the push of their current pain and the pull of your solution. And two things hold them back: their anxiety about the new thing and their attachment to the old, familiar mess. Good messaging turns up the first two and, crucially, actively defuses the last two. Most B2B copy only ever pushes. It never addresses the fear of switching, which is usually the actual reason deals stall.

![Two colleagues in a bright meeting room leaning over a notebook together, one gesturing while the other listens intently, mid-conversation](../../assets/blog/buy-the-problem-02.webp)

Ask what "good" looked like to them. Not the feature they wanted. The outcome that would let them exhale. When someone says "I just needed to walk into the QBR without getting ambushed," that sentence is worth more than your entire feature matrix. That's the headline. That's the job. Write that down verbatim, because your customer just wrote your homepage for you and did it better than any copywriter would.

Then rewrite. Lead with the problem and the outcome. Let the buyer see themselves in the first line, recognize their own situation, feel understood before you've asked them for anything. And then, only then, let the product show up. Not as the hero of the story but as the answer to a question you've already made them feel. The features don't disappear. They move to where they belong: as proof, arriving after the buyer already wants the outcome, not before they know why they should care.

## Sell the Hole, Not the Drill

The reframe is simple to say and genuinely hard to do, because everyone in your building is in love with the drill. You built it. You know every setting. Of course you want to talk about it. But the buyer is standing in front of a wall thinking about the hole, and every sentence you spend admiring your own craftsmanship is a sentence that doesn't reach them.

Go read your own homepage one more time with a single question in mind: does the first thing a stranger sees describe my product, or their problem? If it's the product, you've built a mirror. Turn it into a window, one they can look through and see their own situation on the other side, with your product standing quietly next to the outcome they wanted all along.

That shift, from what you made to what it makes possible, is the whole game. It's the difference between being a line on a spreadsheet and being the obvious answer to a question the buyer already can't stop asking.
