01 Context & Origin

The B2B Tinkerers was born from a personal and professional reality. After 12+ years leading marketing teams in B2B industrial and technology companies — including THIELMANN and Mimacom/Flowable — the founder identified a persistent gap in the market.

01
Missing strategic leadership

Most B2B companies of medium size have marketing teams but no strategic leadership. Activities happen, but without direction.

02
Agency vs. insider perspective

External agencies bring an agency perspective, not a business one. They optimise for deliverables, not for outcomes.

03
Activity without impact

Marketing activity rarely connects to business outcomes. Campaigns run, budgets are spent, but the bridge to revenue is missing.

'Tinkerers' is plural by design — the vision is a firm where multiple senior practitioners, each with deep insider experience, serve clients across markets. The founder is the first Tinkerer, but not the last.

02 Who We Serve

We work with mid-market B2B companies in tech/SaaS and industrial sectors that already have a functioning marketing structure — team, budget, tools — but where nobody is asking the hard question: is any of this actually working?

The company

A B2B tech, SaaS, or industrial firm — machinery, automation, robotics, supply chain — with 100–500 employees, a marketing team of 3–10 people, and an annual marketing budget that gets renewed without serious scrutiny. Revenue €20M–€200M.

The situation

Marketing runs, budget is approved, activities happen. But the CEO and Sales Director have a nagging sense that the return isn't there — and nobody inside has the seniority or the distance to challenge it honestly.

The two people who feel it most
01
The CEO who suspects marketing is on autopilot

The marketing budget gets approved every year. The team delivers the plan. But when the CEO sits across from the Sales Director and the pipeline conversation comes up, marketing isn't part of the answer. They're not looking for a new agency — they're looking for someone who can tell them, from experience, what's actually wrong and how to fix it.

02
The Sales Director who has given up on marketing

In most mid-market B2B companies, marketing reports to sales or directly to the CEO. The Sales Director has tried to align marketing with commercial priorities — and eventually stopped trying. They've built workarounds. They've stopped including marketing in pipeline conversations. When they push for external help, it's not to improve marketing — it's to finally get something useful out of it.

Who we are NOT for
✕

Early-stage startups with no commercial traction — there's no structure to optimise yet.

✕

Companies that want an agency to produce more content, run more campaigns, or fill a headcount gap.

✕

Large enterprises with a fully-staffed CMO function and a defined marketing strategy — they have internal resources for this.

✕

Companies that are not ready to question what they're doing — our value comes from honest diagnosis, and that requires openness.

Buyer profiles — the people we talk to
A
The CEO — the authority and the decision maker

Built the company through product and sales. Marketing has always been a cost centre they tolerate rather than understand. They've approved the budget for years. Now something has shifted — a board question, a missed target, a competitor gaining ground — and they want to know whether their marketing investment is actually doing anything.

They are not looking for a consultant with a slide deck. They want someone who has managed marketing at their scale, understands the internal dynamics of a company like theirs, and can tell them the truth without a commercial agenda.

Core fear: Discovering they've been wasting budget for years. Core desire: An honest external view from someone who has been there — and a clear path forward.

B
The Sales Director — the internal pressure point

Quota-driven and commercially focused. Marketing sits under them or alongside them, and the relationship is politely dysfunctional. They've stopped expecting leads from marketing — they've built their own pipeline process instead. But the budget conversation comes up every year, and they're tired of defending a department they can't point to results from.

They're often the first to push for external help — not because they want to improve marketing, but because they're done with the status quo. They want someone to come in, assess the situation honestly, and either fix it or confirm what they've suspected.

Core fear: Missing targets quarter after quarter while marketing continues on autopilot. Core desire: Marketing that actually generates pipeline — or clarity on why it can't.

C
The Head of Marketing — the internal champion (handle carefully)

Skilled at execution. Stretched thin. Has been running the same plan for 2–3 years because nobody has asked them to do differently. They may feel threatened by an external senior voice — or they may be relieved. It depends entirely on framing.

We come in alongside them, not above them. The positioning with this profile is: we give them the strategic air cover and the senior credibility to push back on the CEO and Sales Director — and to finally get the resources and direction they need.

Core fear: Being exposed as the reason marketing isn't working, when the real problem is structural. Core desire: A senior partner who makes their work land — and helps them have the conversations they can't have alone.

Trigger events — what breaks the inertia
Key insight

Mid-market companies in inertia don't search for help proactively — something has to crack the pattern. These are the moments when the CEO or Sales Director becomes open to an external voice. Our content and presence needs to be there when these moments happen.

T1
The annual budget review that finally gets uncomfortable

The CEO or CFO asks what the marketing budget returned last year. The answer is activity data, not business outcomes. The silence in that meeting is the trigger. Urgency: high.

"We've been spending €500K a year on marketing and I genuinely don't know what we got for it."

T2
A growth ambition that the current setup can't support

New geography, new vertical, new segment, post-acquisition integration. The company has decided to grow in a direction the existing marketing team and playbook weren't built for. They need strategic thinking before they start spending. Urgency: medium-high.

"We want to enter the German market but our current approach won't translate."

T3
A competitive shift that makes the status quo untenable

A competitor has moved, a new entrant has appeared, or the sales team is consistently losing deals to someone the CEO hadn't taken seriously. The question becomes: are we positioned correctly? Is the market even aware of who we are now? Urgency: medium.

"We keep losing deals to [competitor] and I don't understand why — we're technically better."

T4
A key marketing hire leaves — or fails

The Head of Marketing departs, or a new one was hired and isn't landing. The gap creates a window — a moment where the CEO is forced to think about what they actually need from marketing before they fill the role again. Urgency: medium-high.

"We've had three marketing directors in four years. Before we hire again, I want to understand what's actually broken."

Psychographic profile — how they think and decide
How they find us

Peer referral first — they ask someone in their network who has worked with a similar problem. LinkedIn content that names their exact situation can create the initial connection. They rarely respond to cold outreach, but they do engage with a recommendation from someone they trust.

What makes them say yes

Sector credibility — they need to hear that you've been inside a company like theirs, at their scale. Diagnostic honesty — they want their situation described back to them accurately before any solution is proposed. A defined scope, not an open-ended engagement.

What makes them walk away

Agency language: "deliverables", "campaigns", "packages". Generic frameworks that could apply to any business. Overconfidence before understanding the situation. Any pitch that happens before diagnosis. Anyone who clearly hasn't run marketing inside a B2B company at their scale.

How they consume content

LinkedIn is the primary channel — they scroll, they don't search. They stop at posts that name their specific situation with specificity and no fluff. The CEO reads less than the Sales Director. Both distrust content that sounds like it was written by a marketing agency.

03 The Problem We Solve

The real problem in mid-market B2B is not that marketing doesn't exist. It's that marketing exists, spends, and repeats — without anyone stopping to ask whether it's still the right thing to do.

Core insight

"Every year we do more or less the same thing, and we don't really know if it makes sense."

This is what the CEO and Sales Director say — not about a failing team, but about a functional department operating on autopilot. The team is competent. The budget is approved. The activities run. But nobody has built the connection between what marketing does and what the business actually needs this year.

01
Strategic inertia disguised as stability

The marketing plan renews every January with minor adjustments. Nobody questions the channel mix, the budget allocation, or whether last year's priorities still apply. It feels like continuity. It's actually drift.

02
Marketing reports to sales — but doesn't serve sales

In most mid-market B2B companies, marketing sits under the Sales Director or the CEO. In theory, perfect alignment. In practice, marketing produces what it's been producing, sales ignores most of it, and nobody has the conversation to fix it. Both sides have adapted to the dysfunction.

03
Budget is approved, ROI is assumed

The marketing budget gets signed off because it always has been. But when the CEO or CFO asks what it returned, the honest answer is: we measure activity, not outcomes. The team can tell you how many leads came in. Nobody can tell you how many turned into revenue.

04
The team is capable but directionless

The marketers are not the problem. They execute well against a brief. The problem is that the brief hasn't been seriously questioned in years — because nobody in the company has the seniority, the distance, and the insider experience to do it.

The real gap

These companies don't need more marketing. They need someone who has run marketing at their scale to come in, look at what's happening, and tell them the truth. Not a consultant with a framework. Not an agency with a proposal. An insider with experience — who has sat in that room, managed that budget, and had that conversation with the CEO.

07 Engagement Model

Depth first, then continuity. Three phases — each with a clear purpose and a natural transition to the next.

01
Audit & Diagnosis

Understand the current state: positioning, brand, activities, team, business objectives. The natural entry point — low risk for the client, high value from day one.

02
Strategic Build

Positioning, GTM strategy, marketing plan, AI & hybrid team setup. Designed for the client's team, budget, and reality — not for a presentation.

03
Advisory Retainer / Fractional CMO

Flexible ongoing engagement — from a light monthly advisory retainer to a more active Fractional CMO role. Senior marketing leadership without the full-time cost. A fraction of a CMO salary, the same strategic impact.

04
Project Execution

The audit surfaces gaps. Some of those gaps require execution — a rebranding, a new website, a platform migration, a campaign build. These are scoped and quoted separately as standalone projects, and appear in the client's roadmap as concrete next steps.

Execution is flexible by design: projects can be led directly with the support of trusted third parties, or we step in as project lead when the client already has their own supplier. The key is that strategic ownership stays consistent — the same person who defined what needs to happen also ensures it happens correctly.

On projects: A project only enters the roadmap if it was identified through the audit — not because it's a service we want to sell. This keeps the client's trust intact and the scope honest.

Martech Review

A dedicated service that sits across all phases of the engagement. Most B2B mid-market companies have accumulated tools over time — without a clear architecture, without full adoption, and often paying for platforms nobody uses. The review brings clarity and ROI back to the stack.

01
Stack audit

Map every tool in use: what it does, who uses it, how much it costs, and whether it's actually being used. Most companies are surprised by what they find.

02
Tool recommendation

Based on the team's size, maturity, and objectives — not on vendor relationships. CRM, marketing automation, analytics, SEO, ABM, and AI tooling assessed against what the company actually needs and can realistically operate.

03
Implementation & migration

From selection to go-live. Managed directly or in coordination with the client's existing IT or agency resources. Configuration, data migration, integrations, and QA.

04
Team enablement

A tool nobody knows how to use is a tool nobody uses. Training is built into every implementation — tailored to the team's role, not a generic vendor tutorial.

On Martech: The goal is never the most sophisticated stack. The goal is the most effective stack for that specific team. Simpler, well-adopted, and properly integrated beats complex and underused every time.

On the retainer: The goal is not to create dependency. The retainer exists to maintain direction, catch drift early, and provide a senior voice when decisions matter.

11 Online Presence — ICP Implications

The ICP definition has direct consequences for how The B2B Tinkerers shows up online. This section translates buyer behaviour and trigger events into actionable decisions for content, LinkedIn, and web copy.

The core content problem to solve
Strategic reality

The CEO and Sales Director in a mid-market company are not in crisis mode — they're in inertia mode. Our content doesn't need to alarm them. It needs to hold up a mirror. The goal of every post or article is to make the right person think: "That's exactly what happens in our company every year." Recognition first. Urgency follows naturally.

Content pillars — the 4 themes to own
P1
The marketing-sales dysfunction — what it actually looks like inside

Not abstract theory about alignment. Specific, recognisable descriptions of what happens in a mid-market B2B company where marketing and sales coexist but don't really work together. The Sales Director who stopped including marketing in pipeline calls. The marketing plan that gets presented in January and forgotten by March. Insider authority, not consulting advice.

Post angles: "Why your Sales Director has given up on marketing — and what it takes to change that." / "The moment in every B2B company when the CEO stops believing in marketing." / "Marketing reported to sales. Here's what I actually saw."

P2
The cost of marketing on autopilot

Make the inertia problem visible and quantifiable. Content that describes the pattern — the same budget, the same channels, the same activities, renewed without real scrutiny — and what it costs in opportunity, not just in wasted spend. This is the mirror the CEO needs. Triggers recognition of T1.

Post angles: "What does your company spend on marketing that nobody questions?" / "The B2B marketing plan that survives every annual review — and never changes anything." / "How to know if your marketing is working — and why most mid-market companies can't answer this."

P3
Industrial and B2B SaaS marketing — the insider view

Sector-specific content that signals direct experience to the exact ICPs we want to attract. Industrial companies are almost entirely ignored by marketing commentary. B2B SaaS content is abundant but rarely written by someone who has done it at mid-market scale with real budget constraints. Both gaps are ours to own. Primary differentiator from generic marketing consultants.

Post angles: "Why marketing in industrial B2B is different — and why most agencies get it wrong." / "Lead generation for machinery and automation companies: what works when your deal cycle is 12 months." / "The SaaS marketing playbook breaks when you're selling to large enterprises. Here's what changes."

P4
The modern B2B marketing team — AI, structure, and what's actually changing

A grounded, practitioner view of how AI changes the marketing function in mid-market B2B — not hype, not fear. Content for the CEO who wants to understand what a modern marketing setup should look like, and for the Sales Director who hears about AI but doesn't know what it means for their pipeline. Supports the AI & hybrid strategy differentiator.

Post angles: "What a 5-person B2B marketing team looks like when AI is part of the setup." / "The tasks your marketing team is still doing manually that don't need a human." / "AI won't fix a bad marketing strategy. It will just execute it faster and at more cost."

Web copy — rewrite implications
What changes with the refined ICP

The hero section needs to address three types of visitors — the doubting CEO, the frustrated Sales Director, and the founder entering a new market. One headline can't do all three. Consider sub-navigation or three distinct problem frames in the above-the-fold zone.

Sector specificity on the web

Industrial companies need to see themselves on the page. The current copy is B2B generic. Adding explicit references to machinery, automation, SaaS, and long deal cycles signals sector credibility faster than any logo.

The Sales Director entry point

This buyer profile is invisible in the current web copy. A dedicated section or path — "If sales and marketing are not aligned in your company, this is where to start" — could be a high-converting entry point for this specific trigger.

Proof and credibility signals

The current copy is argument-heavy, proof-light. Even without named case studies, adding specific situations ("we helped a €20M automation company rebuild their marketing function after losing their Head of Marketing") builds credibility faster than positioning statements.

The governing principle for online presence

The right person should read the first line and feel like we wrote it about them. The wrong person should quickly understand we're not for them. Both outcomes are good. Broad appeal is the enemy of this brand.
