Brand Identity
1.1 — Who We Are

The anti-agency.

We are the strategic marketing firm that thinks like an insider. Not an agency with resources to sell. Not a consultant with a generic framework. A firm that has sat in your chair.

What we are
Insiders. Not outsiders.

We've led marketing from the inside. We know the real constraints, the politics, the budget reality.

What we do
Connect marketing to the business.

Not more activity. A clear bridge between what marketing does and what the business needs.

What we leave behind

Every engagement is designed to leave the client more capable, not more reliant on us.

1.2 — Brand Values

What we stand for.

Five principles that guide every decision — from how we write to how we work.

01

2.2 — Colour Usage Rules

How to apply the palette.

Do

Use Forest as the dominant dark background for hero sections, navigation, and high-impact moments.

Don't

Use Lime as a background for large sections — it overwhelms. Reserve it for CTAs, accents, and highlights only.

Do

Alternate Forest dark sections with Cream light sections for rhythm and readability across longer pages.

Don't

Stack multiple Forest sections back to back without visual relief — the page loses hierarchy and energy.

Do

Use Lime exclusively for the primary CTA button, key data points, and the accent on the logo wordmark.

Don't

Use Lime for body text or secondary UI elements. Its power comes from restraint.

Accessibility: All text/background combinations meet WCAG AA. Forest on Cream: 8.2:1. Lime on Forest: 7.1:1. Never use Cream text on Lime — it fails contrast standards.

4.2 — Logo Rules

Protect the mark.

Do

Always use "The B2B Tinkerers" in full — three words, consistent capitalisation, never abbreviated.

Don't

Abbreviate to "B2BT" or any other short form. Never use an icon in place of the wordmark.

Do

Maintain clear space equal to the cap-height of the wordmark on all four sides. Never crowd the logo.

Don't

Alter the typeface, weight, spacing, or colour. Never rotate, skew, or stretch the wordmark.

Do

Use the approved colour variants only: full colour on light, all cream on dark, all forest on Lime.

Don't

Place the logo on photographic backgrounds or any surface that compromises legibility.

5.2 — Voice in Practice

Good vs. bad examples.

✓ This is how we sound
"Your marketing budget isn't the problem. How you use it is."

Direct, challenger. Makes the CEO stop and think.

✕ Not like this
"We offer comprehensive B2B marketing solutions tailored to your unique business needs."

Generic agency language. Says nothing. Could be any firm.

✓ This is how we sound
"We don't arrive with answers. We arrive with the right questions."

Humble but confident. Differentiates from consultants who sell pre-packaged frameworks.

✕ Not like this
"Our proven methodology leverages best-in-class synergies to drive ROI across your marketing funnel."

Jargon overload. No CEO trusts someone who writes like this.

✓ This is how we sound
"30 minutes. No pitch. Just an honest conversation."

Short, warm, removes friction. Feels like a person, not a sales funnel.

✕ Not like this
"Schedule a complimentary discovery session to explore how our services can add value to your organisation."

Corporate and stiff. Immediately triggers "this is a sales call" alarm.

5.3 — Writing Rules

The short version of our style guide.

RuleWhyExample
Short sentences. One idea per sentence.Easier to read. More impact. Forces clarity."We don't do more marketing. We do marketing that works."
No jargon. If your CEO's PA wouldn't understand it, rewrite it.Jargon signals insecurity. Clarity signals confidence."Pipeline" yes. "Revenue velocity optimisation" no.
We over I. Always write as the firm.Builds the brand, not personal dependency."We come in as insiders" not "I come in as an insider"
Active voice. Subject does the thing.Active is confident. Passive is evasive."We build the strategy" not "The strategy is built"
Specific over vague. Numbers, names, outcomes.Specificity is credibility."65% of B2B teams are misaligned" not "many companies struggle"
End on the point. Last sentence carries most weight.People remember the ending."That's where we come in." — simple, confident close.

Image System
7.1 — Editorial Risograph

Tactile. Printed. Human.

All blog and editorial images use a risograph print aesthetic — two-colour registration offset, grain texture, flat fills, warm imperfection. Think The Economist illustration department meets an independent European design studio. Not tech startup. Not consulting slide deck.

7.2 — Image Colour Rules

Strict palette. No exceptions.

The image palette mirrors the brand palette exactly. Every element in every image is assigned a role — there is no improvisation on colour.

RoleNameHexUsage in images
BackgroundCream#F5F0E8Always the dominant base. Fills 60–70% of frame.
PrimaryForest Green#1C4A3AMain shapes, figures, machines, structures.
DarkDeep Forest#142E24Shadows, secondary structural elements.
Riso offsetLime#D4E84ARegistration offset shadows only. Never dominant fill.
Single accentRed#C8372DONE element per image only. Non-negotiable.
The red rule is absolute. One image, one red element — a price tag, a button, a connector dot, a head marker. If two red elements appear in a generated image, regenerate without exception. The lime green functions as the risograph second-colour pass: it appears slightly misaligned from the main green shapes, simulating the imprecise registration of a two-colour riso print. It should feel like a printing imperfection, not a design decision.
7.3 — Texture & Composition

Print quality. Editorial framing.

Texture rules
  • Grain texture visible across the entire image — 15–25% opacity noise over all elements
  • Ink imperfections: slight blotches, uneven distribution, small bleed dots
  • Registration offset: lime layer appears 3–5px shifted from main green shapes, especially on edges
  • No gradients. No glows. No lens flares. All fills are flat or lightly textured.
  • No drop shadows — depth is created through colour offset, not blur
Composition rules
  • 16:9 ratio always for blog images
  • Cream background dominant — at least 50% negative cream space
  • Single focal point — one metaphor, well executed
  • No faces, no identifiable people — abstract silhouettes only
  • Left-to-right narrative when the image tells a process or before/after story
7.4 — What to Show. What to Avoid.

Anti-patterns are explicit.

Do

Use abstract metaphorical scenes. The image's job is to make the article's central argument visible — without words.

Don't

Use photorealistic photography, 3D renders with gradients, or stock illustration style. The brand lives in the editorial world.

Do

Keep the palette to Forest, Cream, Lime and one Red accent. Registration offset should feel like a printing imperfection.

Don't

Use dark backgrounds — Forest Green or black should never fill the entire frame. The cream must dominate.

Do

Apply grain texture consistently at 15–25% noise. Contemporary editorial feel — the brand is not retro or vintage.

Don't

Use vintage, sepia, or distressed aesthetics. No old paper, no antiqued effects — the risograph aesthetic is modern, not nostalgic.

Do

Keep human figures as faceless abstract silhouettes. Posture and action communicate intent — identity does not.

Don't

Show faces, hands with detail, or identifiable clothing. Multiple accent colours (two reds, red plus orange) are never acceptable.

7.5 — Metaphor Categories

Every image carries one idea.

Before writing a prompt, define the metaphor the image will carry. The three categories below cover the full range of B2B Tinkerers content — identify which applies, then define the one object that represents the system and the one that represents the human or output.

Category A
Scale Contrast

Something enormous producing something tiny — or vice versa. Works for articles about complexity, overhead, inefficiency, overengineering. Example: a massive industrial machine producing a single webpage.

Category B
Hidden Structure

What is visible versus what is underneath. Works for articles about hidden costs, real versus stated motives, what is not being said. Example: an iceberg with a brick foundation visible below the waterline.

Category C
Human + System

A small human figure in relationship to a larger system — pushing it, orbiting it, directing it. Works for articles about teams, AI, marketing operations, strategy. Example: one person at the centre of multiple AI agents in orbit.
